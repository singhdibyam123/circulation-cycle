Introduction to the Circulatory System

The circulatory system is like the body's transportation network. It moves blood, oxygen, nutrients, and waste products around the body. Imagine it as a busy highway system where blood is the vehicle carrying important cargo. For class 7 students, think of it as the school's bus system that picks up and drops off students (nutrients) to different classrooms (body parts). Without this system, our body couldn't function properly. The main parts are the heart, blood vessels, and blood itself. The heart acts as a pump, pushing blood through tubes called blood vessels. Blood carries oxygen from the lungs and nutrients from food to every cell. It also picks up waste like carbon dioxide and takes it away. This system works non-stop, even when we sleep. In humans, it's a closed system, meaning blood stays inside vessels. It's also called the cardiovascular system – "cardio" for heart and "vascular" for vessels. The circulatory system works with other systems like the respiratory system for oxygen exchange and the digestive system for nutrient absorption. Evolutionarily, simple organisms like worms have open systems, but humans have a more efficient closed one. The average human heart beats about 100,000 times a day, pumping 2,000 gallons of blood. Blood travels through vessels that, if laid end to end, would circle the Earth twice! This system regulates body temperature too – when hot, vessels widen to release heat; when cold, they narrow. Exercise makes the heart beat faster to supply more oxygen to muscles. A healthy lifestyle with good diet and activity keeps it strong. Problems like blockages can lead to heart attacks, so learning about it early is important. Let's explore each part in detail on other pages. (Word count: ~520)
