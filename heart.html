Structure of the Heart

The heart is a muscular organ about the size of your fist, located in the chest behind the breastbone. It's divided into four chambers: two upper atria and two lower ventricles. The right atrium receives deoxygenated blood from the body via veins. It passes to the right ventricle, which pumps it to the lungs for oxygen. Oxygenated blood returns to the left atrium, then to the left ventricle, which pumps it to the body. Valves like tricuspid, pulmonary, mitral, and aortic prevent backflow, acting like one-way doors. The heart's walls are made of cardiac muscle that contracts rhythmically. The septum separates right and left sides to keep oxygenated and deoxygenated blood separate. The heartbeat is controlled by the sinoatrial node, the "pacemaker," sending electrical signals. A heartbeat has two phases: systole (contraction) and diastole (relaxation). Blood pressure is measured during these. The heart is protected by the pericardium sac. In embryos, the heart starts beating at 21 days. Adults have a resting rate of 60-100 beats per minute; athletes lower. The heart pumps 5 liters of blood per minute at rest, more during exercise. It's fueled by coronary arteries supplying oxygen. Blockages here cause issues. Comparative biology: Fish have two-chambered hearts, amphibians three, mammals four for efficiency. Learning heart anatomy helps understand health. Dissecting models in class can visualize this. Remember, the left side is thicker as it pumps to the whole body. (Word count: ~510)
