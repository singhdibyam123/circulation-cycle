Blood Vessels

Blood vessels are tubes that carry blood throughout the body. There are three types: arteries, veins, and capillaries. Arteries carry oxygenated blood away from the heart under high pressure, so they have thick, elastic walls. The aorta is the largest artery. Veins carry deoxygenated blood back to the heart; they have thinner walls and valves to prevent backflow against gravity. The vena cava is the largest vein. Capillaries are tiny vessels connecting arteries and veins, where exchange of oxygen, nutrients, and waste happens. Their walls are one cell thick for diffusion. The total length of vessels is over 60,000 miles. Arteries branch into arterioles, then capillaries; venules lead to veins. In the lungs, pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood (exception). Vessels adapt: dilate (vasodilation) for heat loss, constrict (vasoconstriction) for conservation. Sphincters control blood flow to capillaries. In injuries, vessels clot to stop bleeding. Varicose veins occur when valves fail. Atherosclerosis hardens arteries. Healthy vessels need exercise and diet low in fats. In class experiments, pulse measures artery expansion. Comparative: Insects have open systems without vessels. Understanding vessels helps in first aid, like elevating limbs for vein flow. (Word count: ~505)
